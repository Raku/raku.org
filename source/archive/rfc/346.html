ABSTRACT

This RFC proposes a rewrite of the Artistic License, called "The Artistic License, Version 2.0". This new license attempts to accomplish the spirit of the original Artistic License, as best it can given that we want the Artistic License to be an open source and free software license, and to be unambiguous. Input from the perl6-licenses@perl.org mailing lists was incorporated as much as possible.

In addition, this RFC proposes that the license of Perl6 be the disjunction of the GNU General Public License and the Artistic License, Version 2.0.

FREEZE NOTES

Not many people spoke out one way or the other about this license. However, I have been over the entire traffic of perl6-licenses@perl.org a few times, and it appears that all the things people wanted the Artistic License to do are addressed, without any known loopholes.

David Grove <lt>pete@petes-place.com<gt> did seem to want the Artistic License to be more restrictive than the version proposed here. I attempted to incorporate his suggestions as much as possible, but did not include suggestions that I felt contradict the other wishes desired by Perl community as a whole.

A copyright lawyer has not looked at this license yet. I sent it to Eben Moglen, but he is very busy and hasn't been able to reply due to lack of time. I hope we find some copyright lawyers to look it over pro-bono and make sure it does what we want.

DESCRIPTION

The Proposal

This RFC proposes two measures:

    • The Artistic License is updated to "The Artistic License, Version 2.0" (or, "Artistic-2.0" for short). This license tries to fit the spirit of the original Artistic License, while keeping the text of the license clear, and legally sound, and address the issues set forth in RFC211.

    A discussion of how "Artistic-2.0" compares with the original Artistic License, and with other proposals are available in the perl6-licenses@perl.org mailing list archives. (See References below for exact URLs).

    • The license of perl6 itself will be a dual-license, the disjunction of the GNU General Public License and The Artistic License, Version 2.0.

Note that due to the limits of copyright law, no copyright license can be written that truly makes sure that "If it's called Perl, it is Perl". This is addressed best with trademark law, and RFC346 makes a proposal on this front.
